Region VI

Scottish Borders & Edinburgh

Miles 330–410

Border abbeys, Reformation battles, and journey's end — where Scotland's turbulent spiritual history meets the Great North Road's final destination

✟

The Scottish Borders were once the richest land in Scotland. Four great abbeys — Jedburgh, Melrose, Dryburgh, and Kelso — stood as monuments to monastic wealth and learning. Then came centuries of warfare, as English and Scottish armies swept back and forth across this contested ground. The abbeys were burned, sacked, and burned again.

What survives is magnificent ruin: roofless naves open to the sky, carved stone weathering beneath Scottish rain. And at journey's end, Edinburgh — where St Giles' Cathedral stands at the heart of the Royal Mile, its crown spire a symbol of Scotland itself. Here, John Knox sparked the Reformation; here, the journey ends.

Mile 350

Jedburgh Abbey

Jedburgh, Scottish Borders

Founded in 1138 by David I, Jedburgh Abbey was one of the great Border abbeys that made the Scottish Borders the wealthiest region of medieval Scotland. The church, largely Romanesque with early Gothic additions, survives almost to roof height — one of the most complete abbey ruins in Britain.

Alexander III married his second wife here in 1285; a ghostly apparition at the wedding feast was later said to have foretold his death. The abbey was burned repeatedly during the Anglo-Scottish wars and finally suppressed in 1560.

Notable Facts

  • Founded: 1138
  • Style: Romanesque/Early Gothic
  • Wedding: Alexander III, 1285
  • Status: Historic Scotland monument

Mile 360

Melrose Abbey

Melrose, Scottish Borders

The heart of Robert the Bruce is buried here — or so tradition holds. Melrose Abbey, founded in 1136, became the wealthiest Cistercian monastery in Scotland and contains some of the finest Gothic stonework in the country. The famous "pig playing the bagpipes" gargoyle looks down from the roof.

The east window, though roofless, remains one of the most beautiful pieces of medieval tracery in Britain. The abbey was destroyed by Richard II in 1385 and rebuilt in Decorated Gothic style.

Notable Facts

  • Founded: 1136
  • Burial: Heart of Robert the Bruce
  • East window: Finest Gothic tracery in Scotland
  • Famous carving: Bagpipe-playing pig

Mile 400

Rosslyn Chapel

Roslin, Midlothian

No church in Britain is more densely carved. Founded in 1446 by William Sinclair, Earl of Orkney, Rosslyn Chapel was intended as a collegiate church but was never completed. What survives — the choir alone — is covered in the most elaborate stone carving anywhere in Scotland.

The "Apprentice Pillar" is among the most famous carvings in the world. The chapel featured prominently in Dan Brown's The Da Vinci Code, but its mysteries long predate fiction.

Notable Facts

  • Founded: 1446
  • Builder: William Sinclair, Earl of Orkney
  • Apprentice Pillar: Most famous carving
  • Carvings: Over 100 "Green Men"

Mile 410

St Giles' Cathedral, Edinburgh

Royal Mile, Edinburgh

Journey's end. St Giles' — the High Kirk of Edinburgh — stands at the heart of the Royal Mile, its crown spire a symbol of Scotland. Here, John Knox preached the sermons that sparked the Scottish Reformation; here, the Scottish Parliament is blessed; here, 410 miles of road reach their destination.

The Thistle Chapel, added in 1911, is home to the Order of the Thistle, Scotland's highest order of chivalry. The crown steeple, unique in Scotland, was added in 1495.

Notable Facts

  • Founded: c. 1124
  • Crown steeple: 1495, unique in Scotland
  • John Knox: Minister 1559–1572
  • Thistle Chapel: Order of the Thistle
