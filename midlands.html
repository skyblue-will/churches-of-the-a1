Region III

Nottinghamshire & South Yorkshire

Miles 120–180

Civil War battlefields, Robin Hood's country, and the edge of the industrial north — where England's history was fought and forged

✟

Newark, Doncaster, Southwell: this stretch of the Great North Road witnessed some of the most dramatic events in English history. Newark was besieged three times during the Civil War, its church spire still scarred by Parliamentary cannon. At Southwell, Charles I surrendered to the Scottish army, beginning his path to the scaffold.

The landscape here is transitional — the flat fens giving way to the rolling country of the Midlands, and then to the first hints of the industrial north. The churches reflect this: prosperous wool churches in the south, Norman foundations at Southwell, and the urban confidence of Doncaster's Victorian reconstruction.

Mile 130

St Mary Magdalene, Newark

Newark-on-Trent, Nottinghamshire

One of England's largest and finest parish churches, with a 236-foot spire that still bears the scars of the English Civil War. Newark was besieged three times by Parliamentary forces; the church tower served as a lookout post for Royalist defenders, and a cannonball hole remains visible in the stonework.

The medieval rood screen is one of the finest in England. King Charles I was at Newark when he received news of the town's final surrender in May 1646.

Notable Facts

  • Spire: 236 feet (tallest in Nottinghamshire)
  • Built: 14th–15th century
  • Civil War: Cannonball hole visible
  • Rood screen: c. 1500, exceptional survival

Mile 135

Southwell Minster

Southwell, Nottinghamshire

The finest Norman nave in England, crowned by twin pyramidal towers that have watched over Southwell since 1108. The Chapter House contains the famous "Leaves of Southwell" — naturalistic carvings of English plants so detailed that botanists can identify the species.

On 5 May 1646, King Charles I spent his last night of freedom at the Saracen's Head inn opposite the Minster, before surrendering to the Scottish army. He would never be free again.

Notable Facts

  • Norman nave: 1108–1150
  • Chapter House: Leaves of Southwell (c. 1290)
  • Cathedral status: 1884
  • Historic event: Charles I's last night of freedom

Mile 160

Doncaster Minster

Doncaster, South Yorkshire

The present church is Victorian — a magnificent Gothic Revival building designed by Sir George Gilbert Scott after the medieval church burned down in 1853. But the site has been Christian since 627 AD, when Paulinus baptised converts in the River Don.

Scott's design, completed in 1858, is one of the finest Victorian Gothic churches in England, with a 170-foot tower that dominates the town centre. The original church had witnessed the marriage of Ivanhoe (according to Sir Walter Scott).

Notable Facts

  • Site history: Christian since 627 AD
  • Current building: 1854–1858
  • Architect: Sir George Gilbert Scott
  • Tower: 170 feet

Mile 150

Blyth Priory Church

Blyth, Nottinghamshire

Founded in 1088, just 22 years after the Norman Conquest, Blyth Priory preserves one of the finest Norman naves in the country. The massive round pillars and chevron-carved arches transport visitors directly to the 11th century.

The priory was dissolved by Henry VIII, but the nave survived as the parish church. A rare 15th-century wall painting of the Last Judgement survives above the chancel arch — souls being weighed, devils dragging sinners to hell.

Notable Facts

  • Founded: 1088
  • Norman nave: Exceptionally complete
  • Wall painting: Last Judgement (15th century)
  • Dissolved: 1536
