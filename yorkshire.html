Region IV

North Yorkshire

Miles 180–250

The Dales, the Moors, and York's medieval magnificence — where the Great North Road meets the spiritual heart of the North

✟

North Yorkshire is where the Great North Road transforms. Gone are the flat fens of Lincolnshire; here the land rises and folds, and the churches rise with it. This is Yorkshire's spiritual heartland — a landscape shaped by monasteries, by Vikings who settled to become Christians, by wool merchants who built churches as monuments to their prosperity, and by centuries of pilgrims walking north to the shrines of northern saints.

At the heart of it all stands York — the northern Rome, where Constantine was proclaimed emperor and where England's largest medieval cathedral still dominates the skyline. But venture beyond the city walls and you'll find treasures equally remarkable: the ancient crypt at Ripon where Wilfrid's bones once lay, the great medieval abbey at Selby where monks prayed for five centuries, and the twin-towered splendour of Beverley, a church that rivals any cathedral.

Mile 195

York Minster

York

The Cathedral and Metropolitical Church of Saint Peter in York is the largest Gothic cathedral in Northern Europe. A church has stood here since 627, when King Edwin of Northumbria was baptised in a wooden chapel on this spot. The current building — 500 feet of soaring stone — took 250 years to complete.

Constantine the Great was proclaimed Roman Emperor here in 306 AD. The Great East Window, the size of a tennis court, is the largest expanse of medieval stained glass in the world. The Five Sisters window contains 100,000 pieces of 13th-century glass.

Notable Facts

  • Founded: 627 AD
  • Current building: 1220–1472
  • Length: 524 feet (160m)
  • Central tower: 235 feet high

Mile 175

Selby Abbey

Selby

Founded in 1069 — just three years after the Norman Conquest — Selby Abbey is one of the few great monastic churches to survive the Dissolution intact. A Benedictine monk named Benedict of Auxerre founded the monastery after a vision led him to Selby, and for nearly five centuries monks kept the hours here.

The building is a palimpsest of medieval architecture: Norman arches give way to Early English lancets, then to the flowing tracery of the Decorated period. The Washington Window contains the arms of the Washington family — ancestors of George Washington, whose coat of arms inspired the Stars and Stripes.

Notable Facts

  • Founded: 1069
  • Dissolved: 1539
  • Became parish church: 1618
  • Birthplace: Henry I (legend)

Mile 210

Ripon Cathedral

Ripon

Beneath the Gothic splendour of Ripon Cathedral lies one of the oldest Christian spaces in England: a tiny crypt built by Saint Wilfrid in 672 AD. For over 1,350 years, this chamber — barely room for a dozen people — has witnessed continuous Christian worship.

Wilfrid brought stonemasons from France and Italy to build his great basilica, one of the first stone churches in the north. The current cathedral is the fourth on this site, its west front a masterpiece of Early English Gothic. The misericords, carved between 1489 and 1494, include a rabbit in a waistcoat — said to have inspired Lewis Carroll.

Notable Facts

  • Crypt: 672 AD (St Wilfrid)
  • West front: 1220
  • Cathedral status: 1836
  • Famous for: Anglo-Saxon crypt, misericords

Mile 220

Beverley Minster

Beverley, East Riding

Larger than a third of England's cathedrals, Beverley Minster is a Gothic masterpiece that never achieved cathedral status — yet rivals any in the kingdom. It began as a monastery founded around 700 AD by John of Beverley, Bishop of York, whose tomb became a major pilgrimage centre after his canonisation in 1037.

The Percy Tomb (c. 1340) is considered the finest decorated Gothic canopy in England. The misericords, 68 in total, form the largest collection of medieval choir carvings in the country. Kings from Æthelstan to Henry V came to pray here, and the Sanctuary Chair still stands — touch it, and you claimed sanctuary from the law.

Notable Facts

  • Founded: c. 700 AD
  • Current building: 1188–1420
  • Misericords: 68 (largest collection)
  • Percy Tomb: c. 1340
