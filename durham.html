Region V

County Durham & Northumberland

Miles 250–330

The land of saints — Cuthbert, Bede, and Aidan — where Christianity came to the Anglo-Saxons and some of Europe's finest Romanesque architecture arose

✟

This is holy ground. On the tidal island of Lindisfarne, Aidan established his monastery in 635 AD, beginning the conversion of the Anglo-Saxons. Cuthbert, the "Wonder Worker of Britain," became the most venerated saint in medieval England; his shrine at Durham drew pilgrims for centuries. Bede, the "Father of English History," wrote his great works at Jarrow.

The churches here are monuments to this extraordinary spiritual heritage. Durham Cathedral — perhaps the finest Romanesque building in Europe — rises from its peninsula above the Wear, built to house Cuthbert's relics. Hexham preserves a 7th-century crypt; Lindisfarne preserves the ruins of the priory where English Christianity began.

Mile 260

Durham Cathedral

Durham, County Durham

Rising from a wooded peninsula above the River Wear, Durham Cathedral is perhaps the finest Romanesque building in Europe. Built to house the relics of St Cuthbert, it has been a place of pilgrimage for nearly a thousand years. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1986.

The ribbed vaulting of the nave, completed around 1130, pioneered techniques that would define Gothic architecture. The cathedral also houses the tomb of the Venerable Bede, Father of English History.

Notable Facts

  • Founded: 1093
  • Completed: 1133
  • Shrine: St Cuthbert (d. 687)
  • Status: UNESCO World Heritage Site

Mile 285

Hexham Abbey

Hexham, Northumberland

The crypt beneath Hexham Abbey was built in 674 AD by St Wilfrid, using stones from the nearby Roman fort of Corbridge. It is one of the oldest intact structures in England — a chamber that has witnessed continuous Christian worship for 1,350 years.

The present church is largely Early English Gothic (13th century), but fragments of the Saxon monastery survive, including the famous Frith Stool — a stone chair that served as the bishop's throne and a seat of sanctuary.

Notable Facts

  • Crypt: 674 AD (St Wilfrid)
  • Current building: 12th–13th century
  • Frith Stool: 7th-century sanctuary chair
  • Roman stones: From Corbridge fort

Mile 320 (east)

Lindisfarne Priory & St Mary's

Holy Island, Northumberland

On this tidal island, accessible only at low tide, Aidan of Iona founded a monastery in 635 AD that would become the spiritual center of Anglo-Saxon Christianity. The Lindisfarne Gospels were created here; Cuthbert was bishop here before his hermitage on Inner Farne.

The priory ruins date from the Norman period, but beside them stands St Mary the Virgin, the parish church, still serving islanders. The rainbow arch of the ruined priory stands as one of the most evocative images in English Christianity.

Notable Facts

  • Founded: 635 AD (Aidan)
  • Lindisfarne Gospels: Created c. 715
  • Viking raid: 793 AD (first on Britain)
  • Access: Tidal causeway only

Mile 280

St Andrew's, Corbridge

Corbridge, Northumberland

Built largely from stones recycled from the nearby Roman town of Corstopitum, St Andrew's contains one of the most complete Saxon towers in England. The lower stages date from around 800 AD — making this one of the oldest continuously functioning churches in Britain.

The Roman gateway arch, incorporated into the church tower, may be the most substantial piece of Roman architecture still in ecclesiastical use anywhere in Britain.

Notable Facts

  • Saxon tower: c. 800 AD
  • Roman arch: Reused from Corstopitum
  • Pele tower: 14th-century fortified vicar's house
  • Continuity: Worship since 8th century
