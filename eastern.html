Region II

Cambridgeshire & Lincolnshire

Miles 25–120

Flat lands, big skies, and some of England's finest wool churches — where medieval wealth built spires to rival cathedrals

✟

Lincolnshire was once one of the wealthiest counties in England. The flatlands that now seem empty were, in the medieval period, thick with sheep — and the wool they produced built churches that could rival any cathedral. Grantham, Stamford, Boston: these were prosperous market towns, and their merchants poured their fortunes into stone and glass.

The road through this region is the old Roman Ermine Street, arrow-straight across the fens. Churches here announce themselves from miles away, their spires rising like beacons above the flat agricultural landscape — landmarks for travellers, symbols of civic pride, and monuments to the wealth of wool.

Mile 75

Peterborough Cathedral

Peterborough, Cambridgeshire

One of the most complete Norman cathedrals in England, Peterborough's west front is one of the most extraordinary facades in European architecture: three vast Gothic arches, 81 feet high, unique in Christendom. Behind them lies an almost perfectly preserved Norman interior.

Catherine of Aragon was buried here in 1536, and Mary Queen of Scots was interred here for 25 years before her son James I moved her body to Westminster Abbey. The painted wooden ceiling of the nave, from around 1220, is the only one of its kind to survive.

Notable Facts

  • Founded: 654 AD (monastery)
  • Current building: 1118–1238
  • West front: Unique triple-arch design
  • Burials: Catherine of Aragon, Mary Queen of Scots (originally)

Mile 90

All Saints' Church

Stamford, Lincolnshire

Stamford, "the finest stone town in England," once had fourteen medieval churches. Five survive, and All Saints' is the most beautiful — a perfect Perpendicular church with a slender broach spire that has become the symbol of the town.

The interior preserves medieval benches, a 15th-century brass lectern, and glass from the same period. The town's wool merchants worshipped here, their wealth visible in every carved angel and painted ceiling boss.

Notable Facts

  • Built: 13th–15th century
  • Style: Early English to Perpendicular
  • Spire: 152 feet, broach style
  • Medieval brass: Lectern c. 1480

Mile 110

St Wulfram's, Grantham

Grantham, Lincolnshire

A cathedral in all but name, St Wulfram's has watched over the Great North Road for eight centuries. Its spire — at 282 feet, the sixth tallest in England — has guided travellers since the 14th century. The church's gargoyles are a gallery of medieval grotesques; its chained library dates from 1598.

Isaac Newton worshipped here as a schoolboy. Margaret Thatcher was baptised here in 1925. The Angel and Royal Inn next door claims to be England's oldest, hosting King John in 1213.

Notable Facts

  • Founded: c. 1180
  • Spire: 282 feet (6th tallest in England)
  • Chained library: Founded 1598
  • Notable connections: Newton, Thatcher

Mile 105 (east)

St Botolph's, Boston

Boston, Lincolnshire

The "Boston Stump" is England's tallest parish church tower — 272 feet of Lincolnshire limestone that can be seen from 20 miles away across the fens. A detour from the A1, but an unmissable one: this is perhaps the finest wool church in England.

In 1607, a group of Separatists tried to flee to Holland from here, were arrested, and were imprisoned in the Guildhall. Thirteen years later, some of them sailed on the Mayflower. The church gave its name to Boston, Massachusetts.

Notable Facts

  • Tower: 272 feet (tallest parish church)
  • Built: 1309–1460
  • Connection: Pilgrim Fathers imprisoned here 1607
  • Named after: Boston, Massachusetts
