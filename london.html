Region I

London & Hertfordshire

Miles 0–25

From the City's ancient stones to the great houses of the north — where the journey begins

✟

The Great North Road begins in the shadow of the Tower of London, where All Hallows has stood since Saxon times. From this ancient church — the oldest in the City — the road winds through Islington and Highgate, climbs the Northern Heights, and descends into the gentler landscape of Hertfordshire.

This first stretch passes churches that predate the Norman Conquest, survived the Great Fire, and witnessed the passage of every traveller heading north: kings and merchants, pilgrims and armies, mail coaches and cattle drovers. These are the churches of departure, the last glimpse of the capital before the long road ahead.

Mile 0

All Hallows by the Tower

Tower Hill, City of London

The oldest church in the City of London, founded in 675 AD — three centuries before the Tower itself. A Saxon arch still stands in the nave, predating the Norman Conquest by four hundred years. Samuel Pepys watched the Great Fire from its tower; William Penn was baptised at its font.

This is Mile Zero of our journey: the starting point of the Great North Road, where travellers have departed for Edinburgh since Roman times.

Notable Facts

  • Founded: 675 AD
  • Saxon arch: Oldest in the City
  • Notable baptism: William Penn (1644)
  • Crypt: Roman pavement survives

Mile 1

St Bartholomew-the-Great

Smithfield, City of London

London's oldest parish church, founded as an Augustinian priory in 1123 by Rahere, a courtier of Henry I who survived malaria on a pilgrimage to Rome. The massive Norman pillars of the chancel survive intact — the most substantial Romanesque interior in London.

William Hogarth was baptised here in 1697. Benjamin Franklin worked at a printing press in the Lady Chapel. The church has appeared in countless films, from Four Weddings and a Funeral to Shakespeare in Love.

Notable Facts

  • Founded: 1123
  • Founder: Rahere (tomb survives)
  • Style: Norman Romanesque
  • Film location: 50+ productions

Mile 1

St Etheldreda's, Ely Place

Holborn, City of London

One of only two surviving buildings in London from the reign of Edward I. Built around 1290 as the private chapel of the Bishops of Ely, its windows hold the largest expanse of medieval stained glass in London. The church is the oldest Catholic church in England, returned to Rome in 1874.

Named for the Anglo-Saxon princess who founded Ely Cathedral, St Etheldreda's sits in Ely Place — technically still not part of London, but a private liberty of Cambridgeshire until 1931.

Notable Facts

  • Built: c. 1290
  • Style: Decorated Gothic
  • Windows: Largest medieval glass in London
  • Status: Oldest Catholic church in England

Mile 20

St Etheldreda's, Hatfield

Hatfield, Hertfordshire

Standing in the grounds of Hatfield House, this church has witnessed some of the most dramatic moments in English history. In 1558, Princess Elizabeth was sitting under an oak tree in the park when messengers arrived to tell her she was Queen. The Salisbury Chapel contains monuments to three centuries of the Cecil family.

The church dates from the 13th century, though much was rebuilt in Victorian times. The Brocket family pew, with its own fireplace, speaks to the comforts expected by the local gentry.

Notable Facts

  • Founded: 13th century
  • Salisbury Chapel: Cecil family tombs
  • Historic event: Elizabeth I learned she was Queen nearby
  • Location: Hatfield House grounds
