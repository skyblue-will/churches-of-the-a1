Region V — County Durham

Durham Cathedral

Durham, County Durham — Mile 260

Founded: 1093
Style: Romanesque / Norman
Visitors: 393,000/year
UNESCO World Heritage Site

Rising from a wooded peninsula above the River Wear, Durham Cathedral is perhaps the finest Romanesque building in Europe. Built to house the relics of St Cuthbert, it has been a place of pilgrimage for nearly a thousand years.

⛪

Durham Cathedral and Castle, together forming a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1986

Durham Cathedral was begun in 1093, just 27 years after the Norman Conquest, and completed in 1133. It represents the apogee of Norman architecture in England — massive, confident, and built to last. The ribbed vaults of the nave, among the earliest in Europe, pioneered techniques that would become standard in Gothic architecture.

The cathedral sits on a dramatic peninsula formed by a loop of the River Wear. Together with Durham Castle opposite, it forms one of the most recognizable skylines in England — and one of the few places in Britain inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site primarily for its architecture.

"I paused upon the bridge, and admired and wondered at the beauty and glory of this scene... I never saw so lovely and magnificent a scene." — Nathaniel Hawthorne, 1857

The Two Saints

Durham Cathedral houses the shrines of two of England's most important early Christian figures:

St Cuthbert

c. 634 – 687 AD

The "Wonder Worker of Britain." Bishop of Lindisfarne, hermit of Inner Farne, and the most venerated saint of medieval Northern England. His body, found incorrupt eleven years after death, was carried by monks fleeing Viking raids before finding rest at Durham in 995.

The Venerable Bede

c. 673 – 735 AD

The "Father of English History." Monk of Jarrow, scholar, and author of the Ecclesiastical History of the English People. His bones were brought to Durham in 1022 and lie in the Galilee Chapel at the west end.

The shrine of St Cuthbert, located behind the High Altar, was one of the richest in medieval England. It was destroyed during the Reformation in 1538, but the saint's tomb remains, and the site continues to draw pilgrims. The treasures that accompanied his coffin — including a 7th-century pectoral cross and embroidered vestments — survive in the cathedral museum.

Architecture

Durham's nave is a masterpiece of Romanesque architecture. The massive circular pillars, alternating with compound piers, are decorated with geometric patterns — zigzags, lozenges, spirals — carved deeply into the stone. These are not mere decoration but expressions of Norman power and permanence.

The ribbed vaulting of the nave (c. 1130) was revolutionary. Before Durham, most large churches had wooden roofs or groin vaults. The pointed transverse arches here distribute weight more efficiently, allowing for larger windows and thinner walls. This technology would define Gothic architecture for the next four centuries.

The Galilee Chapel, added at the west end around 1175, offers a striking contrast — delicate, almost Moorish arches in a lighter Transitional style. It houses Bede's tomb and a rare 12th-century wall painting of St Cuthbert.

The Road Connection

Durham has sat astride the Great North Road since Roman times. The medieval city grew wealthy from pilgrims travelling to Cuthbert's shrine, and the Prince-Bishops of Durham controlled the surrounding lands as a virtually independent state — a buffer between England and Scotland.

Today the A1(M) passes just east of the city. From the motorway, the cathedral towers are visible across the valley — a glimpse that has drawn travellers aside for a thousand years.
