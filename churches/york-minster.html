York Minster is not merely a cathedral. It is a statement ‚Äî of power, of faith, of the long continuity of English Christianity. The site has been sacred for nearly 2,000 years, and the building itself represents 250 years of continuous construction, from 1220 to 1472.

Where an Emperor Was Made

In 306 AD, the Roman Emperor Constantius I died in York ‚Äî then called Eboracum. His son, present with the army, was immediately proclaimed emperor by the legions. That son was Constantine, later Constantine the Great, the first Christian emperor of Rome.

Constantine would go on to legalise Christianity throughout the Roman Empire, convene the Council of Nicaea, and found Constantinople. But his imperial career began here, in a military fortress in the north of Britain. A statue of Constantine sits outside the Minster today, marking the spot where the Christian world was arguably born.

The Great East Window

Britain's Largest Medieval Window

2,000 sq ft

The Great East Window, created between 1405 and 1408 by master glazier John Thornton of Coventry, depicts the beginning and end of the world ‚Äî from Genesis to Revelation. At the size of a tennis court, it contains 117 panels and over 1 million pieces of glass. A recent 10-year restoration project cleaned and conserved every piece.

The window is overwhelming in person. Standing in the choir, the east end of the cathedral is a wall of light and colour ‚Äî blues, reds, golds depicting the Creation, the Fall, the lives of saints, and the Apocalypse. John Thornton was paid ¬£56 for the work (and a ¬£10 bonus for finishing on time), equivalent to several years' wages for a skilled craftsman.

The Building

York Minster is 524 feet long and 249 feet wide at the transepts ‚Äî one of the largest medieval buildings in Europe. The central tower rises 197 feet; the western towers reach 174 feet. Inside, the nave is 100 feet high, its ribbed vaulting creating a sense of infinite vertical space.

The Chapter House, completed around 1290, is one of the finest in England ‚Äî an octagonal space with no central pillar, its ceiling supported by ingenious medieval engineering. The windows are filled with grisaille glass (grey-toned patterned glass), creating an ethereal silver light.

The Road Connection

York has been a crossroads since Roman times. The A1 (following the line of the Roman Ermine Street and Dere Street) passes to the west of the city, but the old Great North Road ran directly through ‚Äî and still does, as the A19. Medieval travellers approaching from the south would have seen the Minster towers from miles away, rising above the flat Vale of York.

The city walls, almost complete, can be walked in about two hours. The Minster stands at their heart, visible from every approach ‚Äî as it was designed to be.
