Region I — London

All Hallows by the Tower

Tower Hill, City of London — Mile 0

Founded: 675 AD
Style: Mixed (Saxon–20th C)
Oldest Church in the City of London

Three hundred years before the Tower of London was built, there was already a church here. All Hallows by the Tower is the oldest church in the City of London — and the starting point for our journey north.

⛪

All Hallows, with the Tower of London visible behind

Founded in 675 AD by the Abbey of Barking, All Hallows has witnessed nearly 1,400 years of London history. The church predates the Norman Conquest by four centuries. When William the Conqueror began building his great fortress next door in 1078, All Hallows was already ancient.

The name "All Hallows" means "All Saints" — the church is dedicated to all the saints and martyrs. "By the Tower" distinguishes it from the dozen other All Hallows churches that once stood in London.

The Layers of History

Few buildings in Britain show their archaeological layers as clearly as All Hallows. Dig down and you pass through 2,000 years:

Roman

1st–4th Century

A tessellated pavement from a Roman house survives in the crypt

Saxon

7th Century

The original arch from 675 AD still stands in the nave

Modern

20th Century

Rebuilt after WWII bombing, incorporating medieval fragments

The Saxon arch, visible at the west end of the nave, is believed to be the oldest surviving arch in the City. Built when England was still a patchwork of competing kingdoms, it has stood through Viking raids, Norman conquest, plague, fire, and blitz.

Fire and Blitz

All Hallows has twice been saved by extraordinary luck — or providence.

In 1666, the Great Fire of London destroyed 87 churches. All Hallows survived because Samuel Pepys's naval colleague, Admiral Sir William Penn, had the buildings between the church and the fire pulled down, creating a firebreak. Pepys himself watched the fire from All Hallows' tower, recording the horrific scene in his diary.

"I up to the top of Barking steeple, and there saw the saddest sight of desolation that I ever saw." — Samuel Pepys, 2 September 1666

Less fortunate in 1940, the church was gutted by German incendiary bombs. The medieval interior was destroyed, but the Saxon arch and some medieval brasses survived. The postwar rebuilding, completed in 1957, incorporated these fragments into a new structure — a church that is both ancient and modern.

Notable Connections

William Penn, founder of Pennsylvania, was baptised here in 1644 — the baptismal font still exists. John Quincy Adams, sixth President of the United States, was married here in 1797. The executed bodies of Thomas More, John Fisher, and Archbishop Laud were all brought to All Hallows after their beheading at the Tower.

Today the church serves the City of London's financial community, offering daily services, a quiet space for reflection, and a remarkable museum in the crypt where Roman and Saxon artefacts sit alongside medieval brasses and Civil War armour.

The Journey Begins

All Hallows marks the southern end of our journey. The Great North Road — now the A1 — began at the edge of the City, just yards from here. Travellers heading north would pass the Tower, cross London's fields, and begin the long journey to Edinburgh.

From this ancient church, 410 miles of road stretch ahead.
